01 · Framework

Pain Point. Decision Trigger. Job To Be Done.

Three questions. Run them against every article before you write the first sentence. The answers tell you what the opening paragraph needs to acknowledge, how deep the article needs to go, and where it should end. Skipping this is why articles get written that no specific person would find genuinely useful.

01
Pain Point
The ongoing professional problem that makes this topic relevant to the reader's life. Not a generic frustration — a specific, nameable thing that's costing them time, trust, or results on a recurring basis.
"Our projects keep running late and I don't have visibility into why until it's already a problem."
02
Decision Trigger
The specific event that made them search today rather than six months ago. Something changed — a deadline was missed, a client complained, a manager asked for a process, a contract renewal is coming up. The trigger shapes urgency and intent.
"We just missed a client deadline and my manager asked me to come back with a plan to prevent it happening again."
03
Job To Be Done
What the reader needs to accomplish by the time they close the tab. Understand a concept. Make a decision. Build a case for their team. Choose between options. Each job requires a different structure, depth, and ending.
"I need to walk into a meeting tomorrow with a credible answer to why our timelines slip and what we're going to do about it."
✍
These three things connect to Sessions 1–3 directly. Every pain point maps to something you now understand about how projects fail — scope creep, poor handoffs, unclear ownership, no visibility into progress. Every decision trigger maps to a real event in a professional's working life. Every job to be done maps to a stage on the awareness spectrum. You're not applying an abstract framework — you're applying the industry context you've built over four sessions to a real person's real situation.
02 · Application

How the Reader Changes the Article

Two writers covering the same topic will write different articles if they've identified different readers. These examples show exactly what changes — and what should change — when you write for a specific person rather than a generic audience.

Topic: "How to manage project scope"
Same topic · Two readers · Two different articles
Reader A
The Stretched Team Lead
Pain pointClients keep adding requests mid-project and the team ends up working weekends.
TriggerA project just overran by three weeks due to late additions that weren't scoped.
JTBDA practical way to push back on scope additions without damaging the client relationship.
OpeningOpens with the scenario: a client sends a "quick addition" at 4pm on a Friday. The reader nods. You've already earned the next paragraph.
DepthPractical steps, real language for client conversations, a simple template. No formal scope management theory.
EndingCloses when the reader has a usable process. No need to explain what a WBS is.
Reader B
The Evaluating PM
Pain pointScope creep is eroding margin on fixed-price contracts and the team can't trace where it originates.
TriggerPost-mortem on a recently closed project showed 30% of work was undocumented additions.
JTBDA structured approach to scope change control that can be formalised across multiple projects.
OpeningOpens with the business cost: undocumented scope additions are a margin problem, not just a workload one. Doesn't explain what scope is.
DepthChange control processes, traceability, how to document and price additions, governance options. Assumes PM literacy throughout.
EndingCloses with implementation — how to roll this out to a team, what good looks like in practice.
Topic: "What is Agile project management"
Same topic · Two readers · Two different articles
Reader A
The Junior Team Member
Pain pointTheir team "does Agile" and they nod along in sprint planning without understanding what they're agreeing to.
TriggerOnboarding at a new job. Their manager mentioned the Agile methodology and they want to understand before the next meeting.
JTBDUnderstand what Agile is well enough to participate confidently — not become an expert.
OpeningStarts from their experience: "if your team works in sprints, holds standups, and reviews work at the end of each cycle — you're already doing Agile."
DepthCore concepts only — iterations, sprints, retrospectives, the backlog. Plain language, concrete examples. No comparison to Waterfall until the concept is solid.
EndingCloses with "what to expect in a typical Agile team" — sends them back to work feeling oriented.
Reader B
The Founder / Ops Manager
Pain pointA developer they hired keeps asking to "work Agile" and they're not sure what that means for their delivery timelines and client commitments.
TriggerThey need to decide whether to adopt Agile for an upcoming product build — a real decision with real consequences.
JTBDUnderstand whether Agile is right for their situation — not a textbook definition.
OpeningStarts from the decision they're facing: "your developer wants to work in sprints. Here's what that means for your deadlines, your client conversations, and your team structure."
DepthWhat Agile means practically for a non-technical founder. When it works, when it doesn't. What they need to have in place before adopting it.
EndingCloses with a decision framework — "if X describes your situation, Agile is worth trying. If Y, start somewhere simpler."
✍
The pattern in both examples: The reader's situation shapes the opening sentence, the assumed knowledge level, the examples used, and where the article ends. None of these differences require writing a worse article for either reader — they require writing a different article. The instinct to cover everything for everyone is what produces articles that genuinely serve nobody.
03 · Limits

What ProofHub's Content Is Not Trying to Do

Understanding your reader also means understanding the limits of a single article. Writing for a defined reader is a deliberate editorial choice — it means explicitly not writing for everyone else at the same time. That's not a failure. It's how good content works.

The core tension
Writing for everyone usually means writing for no one. Choosing a reader is not about excluding people — it's about giving one person a genuinely useful experience instead of giving many people a vaguely relevant one.
Not its job
Convert every reader into a ProofHub user in one visit
Most readers are not ready to buy when they arrive. A Stage 1 reader finding your article on "what is a project kickoff meeting" is building awareness, not evaluating tools. Content that pushes ProofHub at that moment reads as intrusive and loses the reader's trust for every future article they might find.
Not its job
Teach every aspect of PM in every article
An article about project scope management does not need to also cover risk, scheduling, and stakeholder communication. Including everything signals to the reader that the writer didn't trust them to know what they were searching for. Serve the search intent — then stop.
Not its job
Argue that ProofHub is the best tool for every situation
ProofHub is the right tool for many teams. It is not right for every team. Content that forces a ProofHub recommendation into every topic — including topics where it's irrelevant — reads as promotional. A reader who feels manipulated doesn't come back. A reader who got genuinely useful information might.
Not its job
Please every reader equally on every article
A deeply practical article for the Stretched Team Lead will feel too basic to the Evaluating PM. That's fine. The alternative — writing it vague enough to not alienate either — means it's not genuinely useful to either. Choose the reader the article is actually for. Write for them. Let the others find the article that was written for them.
Not its job
Resolve every question the reader might have
An article that tries to cover every related question becomes a sprawling resource that answers none of them well. Define the job to be done for this specific reader. Do that job. End the article when the job is done — not when you've run out of related things to say.
Not its job
Sound like it was written by a PM expert for PM experts
Most of ProofHub's readers are not certified PMs. They're professionals who manage projects as part of a broader role. Content written in dense PM vocabulary — process groups, knowledge areas, integrated baselines — excludes the majority of people who would genuinely benefit from a clearer explanation in plain language.
✍
The editorial principle behind all of this: Every time you sit down to write, you are making a choice about who the article is for. That choice should be conscious and deliberate — not defaulted into by trying to include everything. A well-defined reader is the single most useful constraint you can impose on yourself before you start writing. Everything else — structure, depth, tone, examples, where the article ends — follows from knowing who you're actually talking to.
