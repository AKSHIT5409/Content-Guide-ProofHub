01 · Foundation

Why Methodologies Exist

Every major PM methodology has an origin story rooted in failure. Teams hit a wall — missed deadlines, budget overruns, collapsed software projects, manufacturing chaos — and someone formalised a better way. Understanding this is what separates a writer who can explain methodologies from one who just lists their features.

Methodologies are not philosophies invented in conference rooms.
They are solutions to specific, documented failures — built by practitioners who got burned and decided to codify what worked. Core principle for Session 3
Industry Framing

Before any methodology existed, teams ran projects on instinct, hierarchy, and hope. The chaos was expensive — construction projects ran over budget, manufacturing lines broke down unpredictably, and early software projects were a catastrophe. Each methodology below is a direct response to that chaos in a specific industry context.

Waterfall
1950s–1970s
Born from: Construction & Manufacturing logic

Waterfall formalised what construction and engineering teams already did: plan everything upfront, then execute in strict sequence — requirements → design → build → test → deploy. You don't pour the foundation after the walls go up. For physical projects with fixed specs, this made complete sense.

The failure it revealed: When software teams adopted this logic in the 1970s–80s, they discovered a fatal flaw — software requirements change, and changing a requirement in Waterfall means going back to the start. Projects that took years to build were obsolete by delivery.

Construction Defence Manufacturing Government
Agile
Manifesto: 2001
Born from: Software development's Waterfall failures

Seventeen software developers, exhausted by failed Waterfall projects, met in Utah in 2001 and wrote the Agile Manifesto. It was a direct rejection of the rigid, documentation-heavy, plan-everything-upfront approach. The core insight: software is not a building. Requirements evolve, and your process needs to accommodate that.

What it introduced: Iterative delivery, continuous feedback loops, cross-functional teams, working software over comprehensive documentation. Agile is a philosophy, not a process — it describes values and principles, not specific steps.

Software Dev Product Teams Startups Tech Companies
Scrum
1990s–present
Born from: Formalising what high-performing teams already did

Jeff Sutherland and Ken Schwaber observed that the best software teams weren't following Waterfall — they were working in short bursts, holding daily check-ins, and retrospecting regularly. Scrum formalised this pattern. It gave names to the roles (Scrum Master, Product Owner, Development Team), the ceremonies (Sprint Planning, Daily Standup, Sprint Review, Retrospective), and the artifacts (Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog).

Why it matters: Scrum is the most widely implemented Agile framework. Many teams say they "do Agile" but what they mean is they do a version of Scrum — often an adapted, impure version.

Software Dev Product Management Marketing Teams
Kanban
1940s Toyota → Knowledge Work: 2000s
Born from: Toyota's manufacturing floor constraints

Toyota's production system was being throttled by overproduction — building inventory nobody needed yet, while other parts of the line stalled. Kanban (Japanese for "visual signal") solved this by making work visible on a board and capping how much work could be in progress at any stage. When one station finished, it pulled from the previous — not the other way around.

The modern translation: David Anderson adapted Kanban for software teams in 2007. The board became columns (To Do → In Progress → Done), and WIP limits became a tool for exposing bottlenecks in knowledge work. Unlike Scrum, Kanban has no roles, no sprints, no ceremonies — just flow.

PRINCE2
1989 (UK Government)
Born from: UK government IT projects losing control

PRINCE2 (PRojects IN Controlled Environments) was commissioned by the UK government after a series of costly, unaccountable public sector IT failures. It defined a highly structured process with clearly assigned roles, defined governance stages, and mandatory documentation — ensuring someone was always accountable at every decision point.

Why it still matters: PRINCE2 dominates in UK/European government, finance, and large enterprise environments where accountability, audit trails, and formal governance are non-negotiable. It is rarely used in fast-moving tech companies — the overhead doesn't suit that pace.

Government Finance Large Enterprise UK / Europe
✍
For your writing: Whenever you write about a methodology, anchor it in the problem it was built to solve. "Agile works well for software teams" is forgettable. "Agile was built specifically because Waterfall was producing software that was outdated by the time it shipped" is the sentence a reader understands and remembers.

03 · The Real World

The Hybrid Reality

No team runs a textbook methodology. Most teams run a hybrid — shaped by their history, their tools, their people, and what worked last quarter. A writer who doesn't understand this will produce methodology content that sounds out of touch to the very readers they're trying to help.

Why hybrids exist — and why it's not a failure

A Scrum team that skips Sprint Reviews because their client prefers continuous delivery hasn't "broken Scrum." They've adapted it to fit a real working relationship. A marketing team that runs monthly sprints and a Kanban board at the same time isn't confused — they're dealing with two different types of work simultaneously. Hybrid approaches are not a sign that a team is doing PM wrong. They are usually a sign that a team is doing PM realistically.

Common Hybrid
Software Team — "ScrumBan"
Uses Scrum sprints for feature development, but manages bugs and support tickets on a continuous Kanban board alongside. Two work types, two rhythms, one team.
Common Hybrid
Agency — "Waterfall client, Agile delivery"
Presents clients with a fixed-scope, fixed-timeline Waterfall plan (what clients want). Internally delivers it in two-week Agile sprints. The output looks Waterfall; the process is Agile.
Common Hybrid
Enterprise — "Scaled Agile"
Uses SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework) to coordinate Agile teams across a large organisation that still has Waterfall-style annual budgeting and planning. A deliberate institutional compromise.
Common Hybrid
Content Team — "Sprint + Flow"
Monthly editorial planning sprint sets the calendar. Individual articles move through a Kanban board (Drafting → Review → Published). Strategic work is planned; operational work flows.
✍
The practical implication for your writing: When you write about Scrum, you will be writing for readers who do a version of Scrum — not the textbook version. Acknowledge this. Don't write "a Scrum team must hold Sprint Reviews every two weeks." Write about the intent behind Sprint Reviews and when teams adapt the cadence to fit their situation. Readers who live in hybrid environments disengage immediately from content that lectures them about doing their methodology "wrong."
Industry Framing — Connecting it to the Reader

The reason hybrid approaches dominate is the same reason PM tools exist: real organisations are messy, and teams need flexible systems that adapt to their work rather than the other way around. When you write about methodologies, the underlying question for the reader is always: "will this work for how my team actually operates?" That question is never answered by a purist description of how a methodology is supposed to work.
