A terminology guide for writers. Not a session to study — a reference to consult when you encounter PMBOK language in sources so you can tell whether it's current or outdated.

Why This Exists

The PMBOK Guide is one of the most widely referenced documents in PM. It gets updated, and the terminology shifts between editions. Online sources don't always signal which edition they're drawing from. This guide tells you what changed in the 8th Edition (2025) so you can read sources accurately and write with current vocabulary.

00 · Context

Which Edition Are You Reading?

Most PM content online was written against the 5th or 6th Edition. The 7th Edition (2021) introduced major structural changes. The 8th Edition (2025) refined those further. Before you use a source for a methodology or PM process article, it helps to know where its vocabulary comes from.

5th / 6th Edition
2013 / 2017
Process-heavy. Organised by 10 Knowledge Areas and 5 Process Groups. Heavy on inputs, tools, techniques, and outputs (ITTO). Still widely referenced — this is what many certification-prep resources are based on.
7th Edition
2021
Major structural break. Removed process groups and knowledge areas as the primary organising framework. Introduced 8 Performance Domains and 12 Principles. Controversial — seen as too abstract by many practitioners.
8th Edition — Current
2025
Streamlined the 7th. Six principles (down from 12). Process Groups reintroduced as "Focus Areas." Seven Performance Domains. Terminology updated to reflect value-delivery framing. Explicit hybrid and AI coverage.
Why This Matters for Your Writing

A source that says "process group" is almost certainly pre-2021. A source that says "focus area" is current. A source that uses "knowledge areas" as the primary framework is 6th Edition or earlier. None of this makes a source wrong — but it means you should cross-check before treating its framing as the current standard. When you write, use current vocabulary.

01 · What Changed

Four Changes Worth Knowing

These are the changes that affect how PM is discussed in current sources, certification materials, and practitioner writing. Understanding them helps you evaluate what you're reading — not memorise a framework.

How "project" and "success" are defined
Reframe
Before (older editions)
A temporary endeavour undertaken to create a unique product, service, or result. Success = delivering within scope, schedule, and budget.
→
8th Edition (2025)
A temporary initiative in a unique context undertaken to create value. Success = consensus that the project delivered value worth the effort and expense.
What this means for your writing: Articles that still define project success purely as "on time, on budget, on scope" are working from older framing. The current understanding is that a project can miss budget and still be a success if it delivered genuine value (the Sydney Opera House example is used in the 8th Edition itself). When writing about PM outcomes, value delivery is the primary measure — constraints are inputs to that, not the definition of success.
Process Groups → Focus Areas
Rename + Reframe
Before (6th Edition and earlier)
Process Groups — five formal categories of processes that a project moves through in sequence:
  • Initiating
  • Planning
  • Executing
  • Monitoring & Controlling
  • Closing
→
8th Edition (2025)
Focus Areas — same five names, but now described as areas of activity that can be implemented through formal processes, informal practices, or flexible policies — not necessarily a strict sequence.
  • Initiating
  • Planning
  • Executing
  • Monitoring & Controlling
  • Closing
What this means for your writing: The five areas are the same. The vocabulary is different. When older sources say "process group," the current equivalent is "focus area." More importantly, the new framing explicitly acknowledges that Agile and hybrid teams don't move through these linearly or through prescribed processes — they address these areas fluidly. This is the PMBOK finally catching up to how most teams actually work.
Knowledge Areas → Performance Domains
Restructure
Before (5th / 6th Edition)
10 Knowledge Areas — each with its own processes, inputs, tools & outputs:
  • Integration
  • Scope
  • Schedule
  • Cost
  • Quality
  • Resource
  • Communications
  • Risk
  • Procurement
  • Stakeholder
→
8th Edition (2025)
7 Performance Domains — broader outcome-oriented areas with 40 non-prescriptive processes distributed across them:
  • Governance
  • Scope (includes Quality)
  • Schedule
  • Finance (formerly Cost)
  • Stakeholders (includes Communications)
  • Resources
  • Risk
Note: Integration and Procurement are absorbed; Procurement moved to an appendix.
What this means for your writing: You will encounter both sets of vocabulary in your research. Sources using "knowledge areas" are drawing from the 5th or 6th Edition — still valid, still widely used, just older. Sources using "performance domains" are current. The substantive content overlaps significantly; what changed is the organising logic, not most of the underlying PM practice. Don't use "knowledge areas" as a current term in your articles without flagging it as historical vocabulary.
Principles: 12 reduced to 6 — Sustainability added
Consolidated + New
7th Edition (2021)
12 principles — seen as overlapping and difficult to apply practically.
→
8th Edition (2025)
6 Principles:
  • Adopt a Holistic View
  • Focus on Value
  • Embed Quality Into Processes & Deliverables
  • Be an Accountable Leader
  • Integrate Sustainability Within All Project Areas
  • Build an Empowered Culture
What this means for your writing: The inclusion of Sustainability as a named principle is genuinely new. This reflects a broader shift in how organisations justify and evaluate projects — ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) accountability is now formally part of the PM standard, not an optional consideration. For ProofHub's readers in enterprise and regulated industries, this is increasingly relevant context. For most SMB readers, it's background — not a practical daily concern.

03 · Application

Five Rules for Writing About PM Concepts

These are the practical takeaways from understanding the edition landscape. Apply them whenever you're writing about PM processes, lifecycle phases, or project success.

01
Use current vocabulary in your writing — even when your source uses old vocabulary
If a source says "process group" and you're writing for a 2025 audience, write "focus area" (or explain both). Your job is to translate source material into current language, not reproduce it as-is.
02
Don't treat PMBOK as the only vocabulary for PM
Most practitioners, especially in tech and creative industries, never use PMBOK vocabulary in their daily work. Use it when it's the right language for the reader. Don't force it where plain language serves better.
03
Frame project success around value, not just constraints
"On time, on budget, on scope" is a valid shorthand but it's not the complete picture anymore. Current practice, and current PMBOK, measures success as value delivered — constraints are inputs to that, not the definition.
04
When in doubt about a source's currency, check for edition signals
The presence of "process groups" or "knowledge areas" as organising vocabulary signals pre-2021 content. "Performance domains" and "focus areas" signal 7th or 8th Edition. Neither is automatically wrong — just signals the edition context.
05
Remember the certification body caveat from Session 3
PMI's vocabulary is authoritative for PMI. It is not the only valid way to describe PM concepts. Practitioners using Scrum, Kanban, or hybrid approaches often use entirely different vocabulary for the same underlying activities — and that vocabulary is equally valid for the readers who work in those environments.
✍
The point of this document in one sentence: PMBOK vocabulary is in transition, online sources don't label their edition, and your job is to write in current language — which means you need to be able to recognise old terms when you encounter them, know what they map to today, and write for an audience that spans practitioners who use none of this vocabulary and practitioners who use it daily.
