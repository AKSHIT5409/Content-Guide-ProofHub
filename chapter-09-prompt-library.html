Gemini Chapters 3 and 4

Run prompts 1–3 in a single conversation for the competitive landscape. Start a fresh conversation (or continue) for prompts 4–8 to build the source list. Never start a new chat mid-sequence — Gemini holds context within a conversation.

1 Competitive Overview Run first, every time
"I'm a content writer at a B2B SaaS company. Research the top-ranking articles on '[your topic].' For each article: what is the main argument, what topics do they cover, and what questions do they leave unanswered? Organise this into a table."
2 Intent and Gap Analysis Follow-up in same chat
"Based on the top-ranking content on '[your topic]': what is the dominant search intent behind this topic? What kind of reader are these articles written for? And what is the most significant gap — the angle or question that none of the top results address properly?"
If too vague "Be more specific. What exact question about [topic] do the top results fail to answer properly?"
3 Search Intent Mapping Follow-up in same chat
"For the topic '[your topic]': what is the primary search intent? Is this informational, commercial, or mixed? What does someone searching this term actually want to walk away knowing? What would make them feel like they found the right article?"
· · ·
4 Primary Source Discovery Source list phase
"I'm writing a detailed article on [your topic] for a B2B SaaS audience. Find me high-authority sources on this topic: academic papers, industry reports, research from credible institutions like PMI, Gartner, McKinsey, or MIT. For each source, include: the title, the publishing organisation, the publication date, and a one-sentence summary of the key finding relevant to my topic."
5 Authority Mapping Follow-up in same chat
"Which researchers, practitioners, or institutions are considered most credible on the topic of [your topic]? I'm looking for people or organisations whose perspective carries weight with professional project managers and business leaders. Include why each one is considered authoritative on this specific topic."
6 Digging for Original Research Follow-up in same chat
"Find research reports, whitepapers, or academic papers on [your topic] published in the last two years. Focus specifically on sources that contain original data — surveys, case studies, or empirical findings. Prioritise PDF documents from research institutions, consulting firms, or industry bodies."
If returning web articles instead of PDFs "Focus specifically on downloadable research documents, not web articles. I'm looking for whitepapers and reports I can read in full."
7 Video Source Discovery Follow-up in same chat
"Find credible YouTube videos, conference talks, or recorded interviews on [your topic]. I'm looking for content from practitioners, researchers, or industry experts — not explainer videos or promotional content. Include the speaker's name, their credentials, and why the content is worth watching."
8 Recency Check After source list is assembled
"Of the sources we've identified so far on [your topic]: which are at risk of being out of date? AI is a fast-moving field — flag any sources older than 18 months and suggest more recent alternatives where they exist."

NotebookLM Chapter 6

Run SEO prompts 9–13 and SME prompts 14–18 in any order — they can overlap naturally. Keep all prompts in the same chat thread within the notebook. After every significant answer, click the citation tags and read the original passage.

SEO Strategist Lens — "What do I need to know to outrank what's already out there?"
9 Topic Coverage Map Run first in NotebookLM
"Look across all the sources in this notebook. What topics and subtopics related to [your topic] do they collectively cover? Build me a structured table: main topic, subtopics covered, which sources cover each one, and how thoroughly."
10 Gap Identification Follow-up in same chat
"Based on these sources, what important questions about [your topic] do the sources raise but don't fully answer? What angles are touched on but not explored in depth? List these as specific questions, not general themes."
If output is too general "Give me specific questions, not themes. What exact question does no source answer properly?"
11 Evidence Inventory Follow-up in same chat
"List every specific statistic, data point, or research finding mentioned across these sources that is relevant to [your topic]. For each one, include: the specific number or finding, the source it came from, the date of the research, and the context in which it was cited."
12 What Competitors Are Missing After loading competitor articles
"I've included several of the top-ranking articles on this topic as sources. Compared to the research reports and expert sources also in this notebook, what important information do the ranking articles fail to include? What do the authoritative sources say that the ranking articles don't?"
13 Structure Recommendation Last SEO prompt — produces draft outline
"Based on all the sources in this notebook — the research, the expert perspectives, and the ranking articles — what structure would best serve a reader who wants a comprehensive understanding of [your topic]? What sections should this article have, in what order, and what is the core argument that should run through all of them?"
Subject Matter Expert Lens — "What do I need to understand to write this from genuine knowledge?"
14 Foundation Understanding First SME prompt — test your own comprehension
"Based on these sources, explain the core impact of [your topic] in plain language — as if you're explaining it to an experienced professional who is sceptical and wants to understand what's actually changing, not what's being hyped."
If any part is still unclear "Explain [specific concept] more specifically. What does that mean in practice for [your specific reader]?"
15 Expert Disagreement Follow-up in same chat
"Where do the sources in this notebook disagree with each other? What is genuinely debated or unresolved among researchers and practitioners about [your topic]? I want to understand where the field has open questions, not just settled consensus."
16 The Sceptic's Case Follow-up in same chat
"What is the strongest case that [your topic framed negatively — e.g. 'AI has negative consequences for project management' or 'its benefits are overstated']? I want the most credible counterarguments from these sources, not strawmen. What would a well-informed sceptic say?"
17 Making It Concrete Follow-up in same chat
"Give me a specific, realistic scenario from these sources that illustrates the most significant change [your topic] has brought to how [your subject — e.g. 'project managers'] work. I want something concrete — a situation, a decision, a workflow — not a general description of capabilities."
If still too general "Be more specific. What exactly was the situation? What decision was made? What was the outcome?"
18 Core Insight Last SME prompt — challenge the answer
"If [your reader — e.g. 'a project manager'] reads only one thing from all of these sources — the single most important insight about [your topic] — what is it? Not the most discussed point. The most important one, even if it's underemphasised in the sources."
If you disagree with the answer "I'd argue the most important insight is actually [X]. Does the evidence in these sources support that? What evidence would push back against it?"

Claude Projects Chapter 7

Set up the Project Instructions and upload your research document before running any prompts. Run prompts 19–24 in order — each builds on the one before. Prompt 24 (the brief) should only be run after all preceding prompts are complete.

Project Instructions Template — Paste before first conversation

Project Instructions Adapt the bracketed sections — everything else stays
I am a content writer at ProofHub, a B2B SaaS project management platform. I am researching and developing an article on [your topic]. My target audience is: team leads and project managers at mid-size companies. They are experienced professionals, not beginners. They are sceptical of hype and respond to specific, evidence-based claims. Your role in this project is: research partner and critical editor. Your job is to challenge my thinking, identify gaps in my research, stress-test my arguments, and help me build a research brief I can write from. You are not here to write the article. You are not here to validate my ideas. You are here to make the research stronger. Never invent statistics or sources. If I ask for evidence you don't see in the materials I've shared, tell me that rather than generating it. When I share research findings, notes, or draft thinking: read them critically. Point out what's weak. Point out what's missing. Point out what a sceptical reader would push back on. Keep your responses direct. Don't over-explain. Don't pad. Get to the point.
19 Gap Audit First Claude prompt
"Read my research findings document. Based on what I've found: what important questions does this research not answer? What angles haven't I explored? What would a thorough senior editor say is missing before I write?"
If feedback is generic "Be more specific. Which exact section of my research is weakest? What specific question am I not answering that [your reader] would want answered?"
20 Stress-Test the Argument After gap audit is addressed
"My candidate argument is: [your one sentence]. Argue against it. Give me the strongest case a well-informed sceptic would make. Don't be balanced — commit to the opposing position and make it as strong as possible."
If the pushback feels too easy to dismiss "That's too easy to dismiss. What would someone who has actually tried [relevant real-world experience] say? Make it more grounded and more specific."
21 Plain Language Translation For any concept that feels fuzzy — run as many times as needed
"There's a concept in my research I can follow but couldn't explain clearly to a reader without jargon. Here it is: [paste the specific passage or concept]. Explain it in plain language as if you're speaking to an experienced [your reader type] who is unfamiliar with this specific terminology. No jargon. No hedging. One clear explanation."
22 The Most Defensible Angle After gap audit and stress-test
"Based on my research findings: what is the most defensible, differentiated angle for this article? I don't want the most obvious angle — every article already covers that. I want the position that is both supported by the evidence and not already saturated in the existing content on this topic. Give me three options, ranked by how defensible and differentiated each one is."
After choosing an option "For option [N] — what's the weakest point in that argument? What evidence in my research would a critic point to?"
23 Evidence Standard Check After angle is confirmed
"Read through my research findings. Flag every benefit claim or assertion that isn't supported by a specific statistic, a named example, or a concrete mechanism. For each one: quote the claim, tell me why it doesn't meet the standard, and tell me what kind of evidence would make it credible."
24 Build the Research Brief Final prompt — only after prompts 19–23 are complete
"Based on everything in this project — my research findings, the gaps we've identified, the stress-tested argument, and the angle we've settled on — build me a research brief with the following structure: 1. The core argument (one sentence) 2. The audience and what they need from this article 3. The content gap this article fills 4. Key evidence — the strongest statistics and findings, each with its source 5. Counterarguments the article needs to address 6. Sections and structure — recommended outline 7. Knowledge gaps still open — what I still need to find before writing 8. Sources to cite — the most important ones from my source list"

General push-back prompts — use in any tool when output is too vague

Too general"That's too general. Give me three specific examples."
Off-focus"Focus only on [X aspect]. Ignore everything else."
Too obvious"What you gave me covers what's obvious. What's the non-obvious answer?"
Too abstract"Give me a concrete example — a situation, a decision, an outcome. Not a description of a category."
Too easy to dismiss"Make the case stronger. What would someone with real experience in this say?"
