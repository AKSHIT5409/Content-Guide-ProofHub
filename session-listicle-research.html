How to Actually
Research a Tool

Listicle credibility doesn't come from how many sources you cite or how long your intro runs. It comes from whether you were inside the product. This session is about what to look for during the 20–30 minutes that separates a trustworthy description from a rewrapped vendor page.

Session Objective

Know exactly what to find during a free trial that couldn't appear on any marketing page — and how to let that detail do the credibility work inside the article, without announcing it.

Section 01

Why the Standard Exists

"Best of" listicles became one of the dominant content tactics in B2B SaaS over 2024–2025, largely because they perform well in search and get picked up by AI-generated answers. The problem isn't the format — it's what most of these articles contain. The pattern became predictable enough that Google and the FTC are now both paying attention.

−49%
Organic visibility dropA well-known $8B B2B brand lost nearly half its search visibility between January 21 and February 2, 2026. Its blog, built largely on self-promotional listicles, accounted for most of the loss.
267
Self-promotional articlesOne SaaS company had 267 "best" listicles on its blog where it ranked itself or its own products as #1. 76 of them had been freshly timestamped with "2026" in the title.
FTC
Regulatory exposureBefore the SEO penalties, at least one company faced legal action for publishing fabricated competitor reviews and ranking its own services #1. The Better Business Bureau censured the company for unsubstantiated claims.
What triggered the penalties

Google's reviews system targets content that implies firsthand evaluation when none exists. The signal isn't the format — it's the absence of detail that could only come from someone who used the product. AI detection tools returned 100% confidence scores on the affected articles. When all eight tools in a listicle sound like their own feature pages, the article fails that test.

This doesn't mean "best of" listicles are off the table. It means the credibility bar has been raised to where it should have been all along. The writers who were doing real research before this happened are not the ones being penalized.

Section 02

The Pattern We Don't Follow

Here is what most listicle research actually looks like, mapped against what it produces. This isn't about other companies — it's a checklist for recognizing these habits in your own draft before it leaves your hands.

What it looks like What it actually is
"I pulled the feature list from their pricing page and their G2 reviews." You've assembled marketing claims. Nothing in the description required you to have used the product.
"I found the tool listed in two other 'best of' articles, so I included it." Circular sourcing. Both of those articles may have done the same thing. None of you have been inside it.
"I gave it a score of 8.5/10 based on features, ease of use, and pricing." An invented score with no methodology. If you didn't use it, there's no basis for any number.
"I described their integrations using the list on their integrations page." Marketing copy. The integrations page says "integrates with Slack." It doesn't say what the Slack integration actually does.
"I put our product at #1 because it's what we're writing for." That call isn't the writer's to make without basis. Placement requires evidence. The evidence is the writer's job to produce.
The line
If the detail could appear on the vendor's homepage, it needs a source citation. If it couldn't — it means you were there.
Section 03

What 20–30 Minutes
Inside a Tool Tells You

You don't need to run a full product evaluation. You need to find the handful of observations per tool that no one who didn't use it could reproduce. These are the six places to look. Sign up for the free trial. The following is what you're there to find.

01
The onboarding experience
What is the first screen after signup? Does the tool walk you through a setup wizard, drop you into an empty project, or prompt you to import data? What does the empty state look like before you've added anything? This is almost never documented outside the product itself.
What this produces in copy
"After signup, Asana walks you through building your first project with a short wizard — you choose a template or start blank, invite teammates, and add a few tasks before the setup completes. The empty state is cleaner than most tools at this stage."
02
Where key features actually live
Not where the marketing page says they are — where they actually are inside the UI. The gap between "has task dependencies" and "dependencies are in the three-dot menu inside the task panel, not in the main toolbar" is the difference between a vendor claim and a firsthand observation.
What this produces in copy
"Task dependencies in Asana live inside the task detail panel — click the three-dot menu, then 'Mark as dependent on.' It's not surfaced on the main task view, so new users sometimes miss it."
03
The gap between the feature name and what it does
"Advanced reporting" might be three pre-built charts with no export. "Unlimited guests" might have a condition buried in the upgrade prompt. "AI assistant" might only work on the enterprise tier. These gaps are invisible from outside the product. They are not on any pricing page. They are what readers actually want to know.
What this produces in copy
"Asana's reporting on the free plan is limited to the 'My Tasks' summary view. The actual dashboard builder — where you create custom charts across projects — is a Premium feature, and the upgrade prompt appears when you first click into the Reporting tab."
04
What the paywall looks like mid-workflow
Where does a free-tier user hit the limit? Does the tool lock the feature entirely, show a greyed-out button, or let you start the action and then block it partway through? This is the most honest signal of how the product treats trial users, and it affects the recommendation directly.
What this produces in copy
"When you try to switch from Board to Timeline view on a free account, Asana shows a full-page upgrade prompt — the Timeline is completely locked rather than grayed out. You'll hit this quickly if you're evaluating the tool for project scheduling."
05
Performance and friction
Does a Gantt view lag when you have more than 20 tasks? Does drag-and-drop in the board view work cleanly? Does the mobile app let you create tasks with the same fields as desktop, or does it drop custom fields? These observations cannot come from a feature list. They require presence.
What this produces in copy
"The mobile app carries most of Asana's core functionality — task creation, comment threads, and due dates are all accessible. Custom fields and rules, however, are view-only on mobile; you can't create or modify them from the app."
06
Integration behavior — not just integration names
The integrations page says "integrates with Slack." That's not useful information. Does the Slack integration create tasks from messages? Does it post status updates back to channels? Does it sync both ways, or is it one-directional? Connect the integration and spend five minutes with it. That's the detail that earns trust.
What this produces in copy
"The Slack integration lets you create Asana tasks directly from a Slack message using a shortcut. Status updates from Asana can be configured to post back to a designated channel. It doesn't sync in real time — updates post when triggered manually or by a rule."

For most tools, you won't find all six in a single session. Two or three solid observations per tool is the working standard. The article doesn't need to announce what you found — it just needs to contain it.

Asana is a project management tool used by teams of all sizes. It offers task management, timeline views, board views, and integrations with popular tools like Slack, Google Drive, and Microsoft Teams. The platform supports task dependencies, custom fields, and automated workflows. Asana has a free plan for up to 15 users and paid plans starting at $10.99 per user per month. Tested version — full example
Highlighted text = only-from-testing detail
Asana
Best for: teams that need structured task ownership and cross-project visibility

Asana is one of the most mature task management platforms available, and the free plan is genuinely usable — up to 15 users, unlimited tasks and projects, list and board views, and basic integrations including Slack and Google Drive. After you sign up, a short setup wizard walks you through creating your first project before you reach the workspace — you pick a template or start blank, add a few tasks, and optionally invite teammates. The empty state is more guided than most tools at this tier.

The feature most teams evaluate Asana for is its timeline view — the Gantt-style layout that makes dependencies and deadlines visible across a project. It is locked entirely on the free plan, not grayed out. When you click the Timeline tab on a free account, Asana shows a full-screen upgrade prompt before you reach the view. The same applies to task dependencies: they're available from the Premium plan upward, and the setting lives inside the task detail panel under the three-dot menu rather than on the main task row, which means new users sometimes miss it during setup.

Reporting follows the same pattern. The dashboard builder — where you create custom charts across multiple projects — is a Premium feature. On the free plan, the Reporting tab is accessible but shows only a summary of your personal task status. The upgrade prompt appears the first time you try to create a custom chart.

The Slack integration does more than send notifications. You can create Asana tasks directly from a Slack message using the shortcut menu. Status updates can be configured to post back to a channel, though they post when triggered rather than syncing in real time. The mobile app is solid for task management and comment threads, but custom fields and automation rules are view-only on mobile; you can read them but can't create or modify them from the app.

For teams comparing Asana with flat-rate tools: at scale, Asana's per-user pricing adds up quickly. A 25-person team on the Premium plan runs approximately $275/month. The feature set justifies the cost for teams that need cross-project reporting and structured workflows, but the pricing model means smaller or budget-conscious teams will hit the ceiling sooner than the feature ceiling.

Key
Detail that could only come from testing the product
Onboarding wizard detail — The setup flow is not described on Asana's marketing pages. It reads as an observation, not a claim.
Paywall behavior — "Locked entirely, not grayed out" is a distinction that requires being there. It's also the detail a buyer wants.
Feature location — Where a setting lives in the UI is only known from use. It's also genuinely useful to a reader evaluating the tool.
Integration behavior — "Creates tasks from Slack messages via shortcut" vs. "integrates with Slack" is the difference between tested and assembled. Both are brief. Only one is credible.
Mobile parity — Custom fields being view-only on mobile is not on Asana's features page. It's the kind of detail that decides a purchase for a mobile-first team.
Section 04

What You Don't Need
to Test Yourself

The standard is not to personally verify everything. It is to own what you claim. If it comes from the vendor's site or public reviews, cite the source and move on. If it implies firsthand evaluation, it needs to be firsthand.

Pull from public sources — cite them
  • Pricing tiers and plan names — Use the vendor's pricing page. Link it or note it as the source.
  • Feature list by tier — Vendor's comparison table. Accurate as of the article date.
  • Integration names — The integrations page is fine for what tools connect. Not for what they do.
  • Customer count and company scale — Press releases, About pages, or verified third-party sources only.
  • Overall sentiment and common complaints — G2, Capterra, GetApp. Note the review count and recency.
  • Founding year, funding, company background — Crunchbase, press coverage, or the vendor's About page.
Own it — this requires actual testing
  • How the onboarding experience works — You have to sign up and go through it.
  • Where specific features are located in the UI — Marketing pages describe features, not navigation.
  • What the paywall looks like mid-workflow — This requires hitting it during a trial session.
  • What integrations actually do vs. what they're named — Connect it. Spend five minutes with it.
  • Mobile app feature parity — Download and check. Don't describe it from the App Store listing.
  • Performance and friction observations — Load times, drag behavior, editor responsiveness. Presence only.
On ProofHub's placement in the article

When ProofHub appears in the list, apply the same research lens you'd apply to any other tool. Placement is not the writer's call — that decision sits with editorial. But the evidence supporting that placement is the writer's responsibility. If you haven't verified a claim about ProofHub from inside the product, treat it the same way you'd treat an unverified claim about any competitor. The fact that we are the publisher is a reason to be more rigorous, not less. That relationship must be disclosed — either in a methodology note or in the article's framing. The goal isn't to hide the bias. It's to produce work strong enough that the bias doesn't undermine the reader's trust.

Session complete

Before you move to writing the description

For each tool in your listicle, you should be able to point to at least two or three observations that came from being inside the product — not from its marketing page. They don't need to be long. They just need to be yours.
