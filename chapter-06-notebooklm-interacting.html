Step 4

NotebookLM: Interacting With Your Sources

The notebook is loaded. Now you have a conversation with it. This is the longest step in the workflow — and deliberately so. The research session is where articles are won or lost.

Tool: NotebookLM — Chat Interface

Two lenses, one notebook

This chapter splits into two distinct phases. They can happen in either order, and they will overlap naturally as you work. But keeping them mentally separate ensures you do both — rather than spending the entire session in whichever one comes more naturally to you.

The SEO strategist lens asks: what do I need to know to outperform what's already ranking? These questions help you understand what the sources say in aggregate, where the gaps are, what data exists, and how to structure an article that justifies outranking what's already there.

The subject matter expert lens asks: what do I need to understand to write this from genuine knowledge? These questions are about your comprehension — not just collecting information, but understanding the topic well enough that when you write, you're writing from knowledge, not from notes.

An article built only on SEO strategy is technically positioned but hollow. An article built only on personal understanding may be deeply written but structurally wrong for what the reader needs. You need both.
Before you start prompting
When NotebookLM answers a question, you'll see small numbered citation tags in the response. These link to the exact source — and often the exact passage — the answer is drawn from. Click them. Every time. Don't just read the answer. Read the original passage it came from. This is not optional — it is covered in detail at the end of this chapter.

The SEO Strategist Lens
"What do I need to know to outrank what's already out there?"

SEO Prompt 1 — Topic coverage map

SEO 1
Topic Coverage Map
"Look across all the sources in this notebook. What topics and subtopics related to AI and project management do they collectively cover? Build me a structured table: main topic, subtopics covered, which sources cover each one, and how thoroughly."
Run this first. It gives you a map of everything your source library collectively covers before you ask any more specific questions.

The instruction to "look across all sources" matters — without it, NotebookLM may anchor its answer on the first source or two rather than synthesising across everything loaded. The table format makes the output immediately actionable: when you see a subtopic with only one source covering it, that's either a gap in your research or a gap in the published landscape. The convergence points — where multiple sources agree — are the established consensus. The single-source points are either frontier territory or underexplored angles.

SEO Prompt 2 — Gap identification

SEO 2
Gap Identification
"Based on these sources, what important questions about AI's impact on project management do the sources raise but don't fully answer? What angles are touched on but not explored in depth? List these as specific questions, not general themes."
The phrase "specific questions, not general themes" is doing critical work. Without that instruction, the output will be vague — things like "more research is needed on adoption barriers." That's a theme, not something you can build an article around. What you want is specific: "None of the sources address how AI tools affect project managers' accountability when an AI-generated estimate proves wrong." That's a question you could structure a section around.

The distinction between "raises but doesn't fully answer" and "doesn't mention at all" is also deliberate. You're looking for questions the sources themselves identify as important but leave open. That is a genuine gap — and it's the kind that produces Information Gain. Write down the top three gaps it identifies. These are your candidate angles.

SEO Prompt 3 — Evidence inventory

SEO 3
Evidence Inventory
"List every specific statistic, data point, or research finding mentioned across these sources that is relevant to AI's impact on project management. For each one, include: the specific number or finding, the source it came from, the date of the research, and the context in which it was cited."
Statistics are the currency of credible B2B content. A claim without a number is an opinion. A claim with a dated, sourced statistic is evidence. This prompt creates an inventory of every piece of hard evidence in your source library.

The date and context alongside each statistic matter because not all statistics are equal. For AI, where adoption has accelerated dramatically in recent years, the gap between a 2021 figure and a 2024 figure can be enormous. By capturing date and source, you make informed decisions about which numbers to use as current evidence and which to treat as historical context.

Watch for the same figure appearing across multiple sources. When that happens, trace it back to the original study. It tells you the statistic is well-established — but also that it's not differentiated. Every competitor article probably uses it too. The statistics that came from your PDF whitepapers and reports are more likely to be genuinely original.

SEO Prompt 4 — What competitors are missing

SEO 4
What Competitors Are Missing
"I've included several of the top-ranking articles on this topic as sources. Compared to the research reports and expert sources also in this notebook, what important information do the ranking articles fail to include? What do the authoritative sources say that the ranking articles don't?"
This is where loading competitor articles last — and treating them as a separate category — pays off. You're asking NotebookLM to do a direct comparison between two tiers of sources: the ranking articles and the authoritative research.

The ranking articles tell you what's already in the conversation. The research reports and expert sources tell you what's available but not yet in the conversation. The gap between those two sets is your opportunity. Whatever the research says that the ranking articles don't include — that's what you can add to make a legitimate claim to being more authoritative than what's already ranking. Read the output carefully. The specific points identified as missing from the competitor articles are the structural backbone of your competitive advantage.

SEO Prompt 5 — Structure recommendation

SEO 5
Structure Recommendation
"Based on all the sources in this notebook — the research, the expert perspectives, and the ranking articles — what structure would best serve a reader who wants a comprehensive understanding of AI's impact on project management? What sections should this article have, in what order, and what is the core argument that should run through all of them?"
Run this last in the SEO sequence. This is where strategic research converts into a structural direction.

By asking NotebookLM to recommend a structure based on your sources, you're letting the research determine the outline — not intuition, not what every other article does, but what your specific body of evidence suggests the article needs to contain. The request for a "core argument that should run through all of them" is asking for a spine. Not just a list of topics, but a through-line. An article organised around a clear argument — say, "AI is changing what project managers are responsible for, not just how they execute tasks" — is more coherent and more memorable than one that's simply a sequence of subtopics. This output is your draft outline. The core argument it identifies is the position your research brief will be built around.

The Subject Matter Expert Lens
"What do I need to understand to write this from genuine knowledge?"

Collecting information is not the same as understanding a topic. You can have twelve sources loaded and still not be able to explain the subject in your own words. There is a specific quality problem that comes from writing an article you don't genuinely understand: it shows up as writing that's accurate but shallow, that moves through information without explaining it, where sentences are correct but no individual sentence could only have been written by someone who actually knows what they're talking about. Readers feel this, even when they can't articulate it.

These prompts fix that.

SME Prompt 1 — Foundation understanding

SME 1
Foundation Understanding
"Based on these sources, explain the core impact of AI on project management in plain language — as if you're explaining it to an experienced project manager who is sceptical about AI and wants to understand what's actually changing in their job, not what's being hyped."
The sceptical project manager framing removes promotional noise. It forces a more grounded explanation: what is actually changing, according to the evidence in your sources, not what vendors are claiming or what enthusiasts are predicting.

Reading this answer is a test of your own understanding. If you can follow the explanation — if you could say the same thing back in your own words — your foundation is solid. If parts of it are still fuzzy, those are the exact areas to explore with follow-up questions. Don't move on from this prompt until you genuinely understand what it's explaining. Ask follow-up questions in the same chat thread. Keep going until you own the understanding, not just the information.

SME Prompt 2 — Expert disagreement

SME 2
Expert Disagreement
"Where do the sources in this notebook disagree with each other? What is genuinely debated or unresolved among researchers and practitioners about AI's impact on project management? I want to understand where the field has open questions, not just settled consensus."
Disagreement is what makes an article interesting and credible. Most content presents a unified view: here is what AI does, here are the benefits, here is how to adapt. What it almost never includes is the genuine disagreement within the field — because acknowledging debate feels like weakness, and most content is written to sound confident.

When you can say "some researchers argue X, while practitioners who have implemented these systems report something different" — that signals you've read widely enough to know the field isn't unanimous. It signals you're reporting on what's actually true, not just what's comfortable to say. The significant disagreements you find here are potential angles for your article. Choosing a position in a genuine debate is more interesting than summarising consensus.

SME Prompt 3 — The sceptic's case

SME 3
The Sceptic's Case
"What is the strongest case that AI has negative consequences for project management — or that its benefits are overstated? I want the most credible counterarguments from these sources, not strawmen. What would a well-informed sceptic say?"
If you write an article that only presents the positive case for AI, two things happen. First, experienced project managers who have actually tried to implement these tools will read it and conclude you haven't. The reality of AI adoption is complicated and often frustrating, and content that ignores this loses the trust of readers who know. Second, you can't defend an argument you haven't stress-tested. If you don't know the counterarguments, you can't address them. If you can't address them, a reader who holds those objections will put your article down.

The instruction "not strawmen" is asking for the real objections — the credible, difficult ones, not the easy ones you can dismiss. Those are the ones your article needs to engage with, even if your position ultimately disagrees with them.

SME Prompt 4 — Making it concrete

SME 4
Making It Concrete
"Give me a specific, realistic scenario from these sources that illustrates the most significant change AI has brought to how project managers work. I want something concrete — a situation, a decision, a workflow — not a general description of capabilities."
One of the most consistent weaknesses in B2B content is the gap between high-level claim and concrete illustration. "AI improves resource allocation" is a claim. A specific scenario — a project manager who used AI-generated risk analysis to reallocate developer time mid-sprint and what actually happened — is an illustration. The illustration is what makes the claim believable.

Your sources — particularly the practitioner interviews, conference transcripts, and case-study sections of research reports — contain these scenarios. When one comes back from NotebookLM, write it down. It may become your article's opening, a section anchor, or the example that grounds an otherwise abstract point. If the scenario is still too general, follow up: "Be more specific. What exactly was the situation? What decision was made? What was the outcome?"

SME Prompt 5 — Core insight

SME 5
Core Insight
"If a project manager reads only one thing from all of these sources — the single most important insight about how AI is changing their job — what is it? Not the most discussed point. The most important one, even if it's underemphasised in the sources."
This prompt asks NotebookLM to distinguish between what is most discussed and what is most important. These are not the same thing. The most discussed points in your sources are probably the ones already saturating the competitive landscape. The most important insight may be mentioned in one source, in one paragraph, and never picked up anywhere else.

When NotebookLM answers this, read it with genuine attention. Ask yourself: do I agree? If you disagree, say so directly: "I'd argue the most important insight is actually [X]. Does the evidence in these sources support that? What evidence would push back against it?" This exchange — where you challenge the synthesis with your own developing judgment and it responds with evidence — is the closest thing to a genuine thinking session this workflow produces. Use it.

The citation habit

This is worth stating again, because it matters enough to have its own section.

After every significant answer — do this
  • Click the numbered citation tags in the response
  • Read the original passage in the source, not just the summary
  • Ask yourself: has NotebookLM represented this accurately, at the level of nuance?
  • Note the source name and passage for your brief

NotebookLM is generally accurate. This habit is not about distrust. It's about two things.

First, accuracy at the level of nuance. NotebookLM might correctly summarise the main finding of a research paper while slightly misrepresenting the conditions under which that finding applies. The original passage will tell you. The summary won't.

Second, building your own relationship with the sources. If you're going to write about this topic with authority, you need to be able to say "I read this" — not just "an AI told me this." Reading the original passage is the minimum standard for having actually engaged with a source.

If an answer has no citations
Treat it with caution. It means NotebookLM is either drawing very broadly from the sources without a specific passage to point to, or it's hedging. Follow up with: "Which specific source is this based on?" and see what it says.

What you should have before moving on

Check this list honestly — not "have I run these prompts?" but "do I actually have these things?"

  • ✓ A coverage map of your sources — what's there and where it's thin
  • ✓ A clear content gap — the specific angle no existing article addresses properly
  • ✓ An inventory of the strongest statistics and data points, with sources and dates
  • ✓ A draft structure for the article, grounded in your sources rather than convention
  • ✓ Genuine comprehension of the topic — you could explain the core argument to someone who hasn't read any of this
  • ✓ The counterarguments your article needs to engage with
  • ✓ At least one concrete scenario or illustration
  • ✓ A candidate for the article's core argument
Time check
This step should take 30 to 40 minutes. It is the longest step in the workflow. If you can't clearly articulate the content gap, go back and run the gap prompt again and push harder. If you couldn't explain the core argument in one sentence to someone who hasn't seen any of your sources, spend more time in the SME lens before moving to Claude.

At this point in the workflow, you have something most content writers never build: a grounded, verifiable understanding of a topic, with the competitive landscape mapped and the gaps identified. What you don't yet have is a tight, organised research brief you can write from. That's Claude's job. It comes next.
