01 · Foundation

Why Personas Aren't Enough

Most content teams hand writers a persona: a job title, a few bullet points, maybe a name and a stock photo. Personas describe who someone is. They don't tell you what that person needs at the specific moment they open your article — and that moment is the only thing that matters.

What a persona tells you
–Job title and seniority level
–Industry and company size
–General goals and frustrations
–Tools they use day-to-day
–A static snapshot of their professional life
What reader profiling adds
✓What problem sent them searching today — not in general
✓How much they already know about this topic right now
✓What they need to accomplish by the end of this specific article
✓What would make them feel the article was worth their time
✓The live context they bring to this page, right now

The same person — a project manager at a 30-person agency — can arrive at two ProofHub articles on the same day in completely different states. At 9am they're searching "how to write a project brief" because a client just asked for one and they've never written a formal one. At 3pm they're searching "ProofHub vs Asana" because their contract renewal is next week. Same person. Entirely different article needed each time.

✍
The shift in how you think: Stop asking "who is this person?" before you write. Start asking "what does this person need to accomplish by the time they close this tab?" The answer to that question determines everything — opening paragraph, depth of explanation, examples, where the article ends.
02 · Reader Groups

ProofHub's Four Core Readers

These are not rigid personas. They are recognisable professional situations that ProofHub's content is most likely to serve. A real reader may move between them depending on the article. Understanding these situations — grounded in the industry knowledge from Sessions 1–3 — is what lets you write for a real person rather than a theoretical one.

The Stretched Team Lead
Most common ProofHub reader

"I'm managing projects but nobody gave me a framework. I'm figuring it out as I go and things keep slipping through."

Who they are
Team lead, department manager, or senior individual contributor who took on project coordination informally. No PM certification. No dedicated PM role.
Their daily reality
Juggling their actual job alongside coordination work. Using email, spreadsheets, or a shared doc as a makeshift project system. Constantly putting out fires.
What they need from an article
Something they can apply immediately. Not theory — practical steps, real examples, and language that acknowledges their situation isn't textbook PM.
What will lose them
Jargon-heavy explanations, content that assumes a formal PM structure exists, or articles that feel written for a certified PM rather than someone learning on the job.
Typical search that lands them on a ProofHub article
"how to keep projects on track" · "project status update template" · "how to manage multiple projects at once"
Practical, immediate, solution-oriented. They know the problem — they want an answer.
The Evaluating PM
High conversion value

"I know what I'm doing. I'm looking for specific information, evidence, or a comparison that helps me make a decision or sharpen my practice."

Who they are
Experienced project manager, operations lead, or department head with formal or informal PM experience. May hold PMP or equivalent. Reads critically.
Their daily reality
Managing structured projects, possibly overseeing a team of PMs. Looking to improve specific areas, evaluate tools, or stay current with practice.
What they need from an article
Specificity and honesty. They will notice vague generalisations and inflated claims immediately. Evidence, comparisons, and acknowledgement of tradeoffs earn their trust.
What will lose them
Over-explained basics, promotional framing disguised as education, or any claim that oversimplifies something they know to be complex.
Typical search that lands them on a ProofHub article
"ProofHub vs Asana for project managers" · "best project management software for agencies" · "how to set up a project governance framework"
Specific, comparative, or depth-seeking. They've already decided what they're looking for.
The Founder or Operations Manager
SMB-heavy

"I'm responsible for everything and I need my team to stop dropping balls. I don't care what the methodology is called — I just need things to work."

Who they are
Small business owner, startup founder, or operations manager at an SMB with no dedicated PM function. They make tool decisions and own outcomes.
Their daily reality
Wearing multiple hats. Projects run informally. Accountability is fuzzy. They're looking to install some structure without creating overhead that slows the team down.
What they need from an article
Practical outcomes and honest ROI framing. They respond to "this saves you X hours" or "this stops Y from happening" more than any methodology discussion.
What will lose them
Complexity, heavy implementation requirements, and content that assumes a team with dedicated roles. They need lightweight solutions.
Typical search that lands them on a ProofHub article
"how to manage a small team" · "project management for small business" · "how to stop missing deadlines"
Problem-first, outcome-oriented. The query describes a symptom — the article needs to diagnose and solve it.
The Junior Team Member
Understanding, not deciding

"Someone above me mentioned this and I need to understand it. I'm not making decisions — I'm trying to catch up so I don't look lost in the next meeting."

Who they are
Early-career professional, recent hire, or someone new to a team that uses structured PM. Not responsible for tool selection or process design — just needs to function within it.
Their daily reality
Being asked to use tools or follow processes they weren't trained on. Learning by doing, often without formal onboarding. Self-teaching through search.
What they need from an article
Clear explanations without assumed knowledge. Plain language. Concrete examples. A sense that it's okay not to know this already — not condescension, but genuine clarity.
What will lose them
Assumed familiarity with jargon, dense process diagrams without explanation, or content clearly written for decision-makers when they're just trying to understand.
Typical search that lands them on a ProofHub article
"what is a project kickoff meeting" · "what does a project manager do" · "agile vs waterfall explained"
Definitional, foundational, or clarifying. They need understanding — not decisions, not tactics.
Why These Profiles Connect to Sessions 1–3

These aren't abstract personas — they're people living inside the industry you've spent three sessions learning about. The Stretched Team Lead is the team lead from the collaboration session who manages projects without formal training. The Evaluating PM is someone who's already navigated the hybrid reality from Session 3. The Founder is running the kind of SMB where PM happened informally long before any tool was introduced. The Junior Member is at the start of the journey you've just completed. When you write, you're not writing for a profile — you're writing for a person you now have enough context to picture clearly.

03 · Awareness

The Awareness Spectrum

Two people can search the same topic and be in completely different states of awareness. The same article cannot serve both of them equally. Before you write, you need to place your reader on this spectrum — because awareness level determines the opening paragraph, the assumed knowledge, the depth of explanation, and where the article ends.

Unaware Problem-aware Solution-aware Most aware
Stage 1 — Unaware
Doesn't know they have a PM problem yet
"how to organise team work"
They're searching for general productivity or organisation help. PM framing is introduced through their actual situation — not through PM vocabulary they don't yet have.
Stage 2 — Problem-Aware
Knows the pain, doesn't know the solution
"why do projects always run late"
They've named the problem but not the solution. They need the article to connect their pain to a cause, and the cause to a practical fix — not a product pitch.
Stage 3 — Solution-Aware
Knows PM tools exist, evaluating options
"best project management software 2025"
They know the category. They're comparing. They need specificity — features, pricing, honest tradeoffs. Generic "here's what PM software does" content is irrelevant to them.
Stage 4 — Most Aware
Knows ProofHub, needs a reason to act
"ProofHub pricing" · "ProofHub review"
They've already found ProofHub. They need confirmation, social proof, or a specific answer to remove the last objection. The article's job is to close, not educate.
✍
How to use this in practice: When you receive a brief or a keyword, place it on the spectrum before you do anything else. "What is a Gantt chart" is Stage 1–2. "Gantt chart software comparison" is Stage 3. "ProofHub Gantt chart" is Stage 4. Each requires a different opening, a different assumed knowledge level, and a different ending. Writing a Stage 1 article with a Stage 4 tone loses the reader in the first paragraph. Writing a Stage 4 article with Stage 1 depth wastes the time of someone who already knows all of it.
