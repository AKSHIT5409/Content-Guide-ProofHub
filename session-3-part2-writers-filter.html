01 · Research

How to Research Methodologies:
A Source Hierarchy

The internet has no shortage of methodology content. Much of it is accurate. A significant portion is not — it's vendor marketing dressed in educational clothes, certification body propaganda, or opinion presented as consensus. Before you write a word about any methodology, you need to know what you're reading.

1
Highest Trust
Primary source documents — the original texts
The Agile Manifesto (agilemanifesto.org), the original Scrum Guide (scrumguides.org), the Toyota Production System documentation. These are what the methodology actually says — before anyone reinterpreted, simplified, or commercialised it. Slower to read, but authoritative.
agilemanifesto.org · scrumguides.org · original PRINCE2 handbook
2
High Trust
Practitioner-written content — people who've applied it professionally
Blog posts, conference talks, and books by working practitioners — PMs, Scrum Masters, engineering leads, consultants who are not primarily selling a tool or a certification. Look for first-person experience, nuanced caveats, and honest discussion of where a methodology struggles. These people have lived with tradeoffs. They write differently from marketers.
Martin Fowler's bliki · Basecamp's Shape Up · practitioner conference talks
3
Useful — with caveats
Certification bodies — useful, but read with their institutional lens in mind
PMI, Scrum Alliance, and AXELOS (PRINCE2) produce accurate methodology descriptions — but remember they have commercial interests. PMI makes money selling PMP certifications; the Scrum Alliance sells Certified Scrum Master training. Their content will present their methodology favourably and emphasise the value of certification. This doesn't make it wrong. It makes it partial.
PMI.org · ScrumAlliance.org · AXELOS.com
4
Read carefully
Software vendor content — educational surface, marketing intent
Atlassian's Agile coach content, monday.com's methodology guides, Asana's PM glossary — these are written by people who know PM well, and the factual content is often accurate. But every guide ends up implying that their tool is the natural choice for that methodology. Read these to understand how methodologies are being explained to a mainstream audience, not as authoritative sources.
Atlassian Agile Coach · monday.com blog · Asana Learn guides
✕
Do not use as a source
Generic "best practices" content with no authorship or methodology expertise
Unnamed listicle articles on high-DA sites, AI-generated methodology overviews, content mills producing "what is Scrum" articles with no practitioner input. These are written for SEO, not accuracy. They often flatten nuance, present opinion as fact, and occasionally mix up framework definitions. You will find them in abundance on the first page of Google.
Nameless roundup posts · AI-generated content farms · scraper sites

Red Flags to Watch For

These patterns in a source should immediately raise your guard. They don't always mean the content is wrong — but they mean you need to verify elsewhere before using it.

Red Flag
The methodology is described as having no real downsides
Every methodology has legitimate tradeoffs. Agile doesn't work well for fixed-price contracts. Scrum overhead is real for small teams. Waterfall fails when requirements change. If an article has nothing critical to say, it's promotional.
"Agile is the modern standard and works for any team that wants to be productive."
Red Flag
"Tool X is the best tool for this methodology"
This is positioning dressed as methodology education. Tool selection and methodology are separate decisions. Scrum can be run in Notion, a spreadsheet, or a whiteboard. Any article that ends with a tool recommendation has a marketing objective, not an educational one.
"Jira is the gold standard for Agile teams" — written on Atlassian's blog.
Red Flag
The framework is described as universally applicable
No methodology works for every context. If you're reading content that argues Scrum can transform any team — a construction company, a law firm, a restaurant chain — without significant adaptation, the author is selling something. Methodologies are context-dependent by design.
"Kanban can revolutionise how any business operates."
Red Flag
Certification is presented as the only path to competence
Certification bodies have a financial interest in making certifications feel mandatory. Many excellent Scrum Masters are uncertified. Many certified PMs are ineffective. Certification demonstrates training; it doesn't certify competence. Content that treats "get certified" as the primary next step is institutional marketing.
"You can't truly lead an Agile team without a CSM certification."
Red Flag
The methodology is described in its pure, idealised form only
As you learned in Part 1 — most teams run hybrids. Content that describes a methodology as though teams implement it perfectly, with all roles filled and all ceremonies followed, is written for textbooks, not for real practitioners. It signals the author hasn't worked in the environment they're describing.
"In a Scrum team, the Product Owner always maintains a fully groomed backlog prioritised by business value."
Red Flag
No author, no date, no cited sources
Methodology knowledge evolves. The Scrum Guide has been updated multiple times. Agile practices in 2024 look different from 2010. Undated content from unnamed authors cannot be verified or contextualised. If you can't tell when it was written or who wrote it, treat it as unverified until you can cross-check the claims elsewhere.
✍
Your practical rule: For every methodology claim in your draft, ask — where did I read this, and does the source have an interest in me believing it? If you can't answer the first question, find a source. If the answer to the second question is "yes," cross-check with a source that has a different interest.
A Note on Certification Bodies

Useful — but read with their incentives in mind

The major certification bodies are legitimate institutions that have done real work to define and standardise PM practice. Their content is often accurate and well-maintained. But they are also organisations with commercial interests — they sell training, exams, and credentials. Use their resources for foundational definitions and process descriptions. Don't use them as your only source, and don't reproduce their framing of "why certification matters."

PMI
Project Management Institute. Publishes the PMBOK Guide — a solid process reference. Sells the PMP certification. Strong US and global enterprise influence. Tends toward structured, traditional PM.
Scrum Alliance
Sells CSM, CSP and other Scrum certifications. Their Scrum definitions are accurate but they have a strong institutional interest in keeping Scrum distinct from other Agile approaches.
Scrum.org
Ken Schwaber's organisation (co-creator of Scrum). Publishes the official Scrum Guide — the best primary source for what Scrum actually is. Less commercially aggressive than Scrum Alliance.
AXELOS / PeopleCert
Owns PRINCE2 and ITIL. UK-government-originated, now commercial. PRINCE2 documentation is authoritative for PRINCE2. Useful for UK/European governance contexts.
02 · Voice

Staying Neutral in Methodology Content

ProofHub's readers are not a monolith. Some run Scrum. Some have never heard the word Scrum and run their projects on instinct. Some are being asked to introduce Agile to a team that's always used Waterfall and is resistant to change. Your job is to help each of them understand clearly — not to advocate for a particular approach or engineer them toward ProofHub.

✕ Avoid — Takes a side or pushes the reader
✕Agile is the modern approach. Most forward-thinking teams have moved away from Waterfall.
✕Scrum's structured ceremonies make it the superior choice for teams that want accountability.
✕If your team isn't using a PM methodology yet, you're leaving productivity on the table.
✕ProofHub supports all major methodologies, making it the natural tool for any approach you choose.
✕Teams that resist Agile are usually dealing with organisational culture issues they haven't addressed.
✓ Aim for — Helps the reader choose what fits their situation
✓Agile works well for teams whose requirements change frequently. Waterfall remains appropriate for fixed-scope projects with stable specifications.
✓Scrum's ceremony structure suits teams that benefit from regular checkpoints. Smaller teams often find the overhead outweighs the benefit.
✓Many teams run projects effectively without a named methodology. The question is whether a structured approach would solve a specific problem they're experiencing.
✓Explain how the concept applies to the reader's situation. Let the tool recommendation follow naturally if it does.
✓When teams struggle to adopt Agile, it's often because the methodology was chosen before understanding whether it fits the work — not because the team is resistant to improvement.
✍
The underlying standard: A reader who prefers Waterfall should finish your Agile article feeling like they understand it clearly — not like they've been persuaded they're doing something wrong. A reader evaluating Scrum should understand both why teams adopt it and the conditions under which it doesn't work well. Clarity serves everyone. Advocacy serves a subset and alienates the rest.
Why This Matters for ProofHub Specifically

ProofHub's readers span construction project managers, marketing directors, agency leads, and software teams. They arrive from very different methodology backgrounds. Content that reads as "Agile-first" silently tells half of them that this content isn't for them. A neutral lens is not just an ethical stance — it's the commercially intelligent one for an audience this broad.

03 · Bridge

Vocabulary to Carry Into Reader Profiling

Session 4 — Reader Profiling — builds directly on what you've covered in Sessions 1 through 3. The concepts below appear throughout all three sessions, and they're the same vocabulary the reader profiling session uses to describe the people who search for and read ProofHub's content. You don't need to memorise definitions. You need to notice these concepts appearing naturally in your understanding of the industry so far.

Pain Point
The specific professional problem that sends someone looking for information. Not "I want to learn about Scrum" — but "our sprints keep overrunning and we can't figure out why."
↳ Used in reader profiling
Awareness Level
How much does the reader already know when they arrive? Are they searching "what is project management" or "Scrum vs Kanban for remote teams"? Same topic, completely different entry points.
↳ Used in reader profiling
Decision Trigger
The event that made someone search today rather than a month ago. A missed deadline. A new team lead demanding methodology change. A tool contract renewal coming up. The trigger shapes the urgency and intent behind the search.
↳ Used in reader profiling
Job To Be Done
What the reader needs to accomplish by the end of the article. Understand a concept? Make a decision? Build a case for their manager? Each job requires a different structure and depth of content.
↳ Used in reader profiling
Reader Role
Who is this person inside their team? A PM writing for their team's buy-in reads differently from a team lead looking for personal understanding. Role determines vocabulary, authority, and what counts as "useful."
↳ Used in reader profiling
Team Context
What kind of team is this person on? Size, industry, remote vs in-office, PM maturity level. As you've now learned — context shapes methodology choice and shapes what content a reader will find realistic vs out of touch.
↳ Used in reader profiling
✍
Your job going into Session 4: You've spent three sessions learning the industry — the problems, the processes, the methodologies, the teams. Session 4 asks: who is the person living inside this industry, what are they struggling with, and what are they typing into Google when they need help? You're not starting from zero — you're applying what you know to specific real people.
