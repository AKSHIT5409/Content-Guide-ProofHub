01 First

Study the Target Publication

Before you research the topic, understand where it's going

Read at least five to eight articles on the target publication before you touch the topic. You're not looking for inspiration — you're building a factual picture of how this publication works. Document what you find. Your outline will be wrong if you skip this step.

Article length range
Count words on three to five representative articles. Note the floor and ceiling. This is your target range — not a guideline.
Structural pattern
Do articles use subheadings throughout, or do they run as narrative prose? Do they use numbered lists, bullet points, or neither? Copy their structure — do not impose your own.
Opening style
Does the publication open with a hook, a statistic, a question, or a direct statement? Read the first paragraph of five articles and identify the pattern.
Tone and register
Formal or conversational? First-person or third? Does the author address the reader directly ("you") or write observationally? Is there humour, or is the tone consistently straight?
What the publication values
Are articles primarily practical (step-by-step, actionable) or analytical (opinion, insight, argument)? Does the publication favour data-heavy content or experience-led storytelling?
How articles end
Do they close with a summary, a call to action, a reflection, or do they simply stop when the content is done? Match the closing convention.
Link and citation style
Do articles link out frequently or sparingly? Do they cite studies and statistics, or are they largely assertion-based? Does the publication link to external tools or keep it neutral?
→
Write down your findings in a short paragraph before moving to Step 2. You will reference them when building the outline. If you can't describe the publication's style in three sentences, read more articles.
02 Second

Understand the Topic

Research the subject through the publication's lens, not in general

General topic research is not enough. You need to understand the topic specifically as it relates to this publication and its readers. Your research has two parts: what has already been said on this publication about this topic, and what angle is genuinely missing or underserved.

Search the publication for your keyword and related terms
Find every article on this site that covers the same topic. Read the top two or three. Note the angles they've taken, the arguments they've made, and the gaps they've left.
Identify what has not been said
Your article cannot cover the same ground as existing content on this publication. Find an angle that's genuinely different — a narrower focus, an opposing view, a more practical approach, or a more current angle.
Understand what the keyword actually signals
Who is searching for this? What do they already know? What problem are they trying to solve? The keyword tells you the topic — reader intent tells you the treatment.
Gather sources and evidence you will actually use
Collect statistics, research findings, or examples that are relevant to your chosen angle. Only gather what you plan to use. Save URLs. Note publication dates — do not cite research older than three to four years unless it is foundational.
Form a clear angle for the piece
By the end of your research, write one sentence describing the specific angle this article will take. If you can't write that sentence, your research isn't complete.
⚑
Do not start the outline before you have a clear angle. An outline built without a defined angle produces a generic article that covers a topic rather than saying something specific about it. The publication already has generic coverage. Your article needs to add something.

04 Fourth

Build the Outline

Structure the piece before you write a single sentence of content

Your outline is built from four inputs: the publication's structural conventions (Step 1), your defined angle (Step 2), the author's voice and content type (Step 3), and the content type logic below. The outline must contain more than section headings — it needs to show what each section will argue or cover and why it comes in that order.

Content Type
Thought Leadership
The article must have a clear point of view. It argues something — it does not just cover a topic.
1.Opening — a concrete situation that sets up the argument
2.The claim — the specific position the author takes
3.Evidence — experience, observation, or data that supports it
4.Implication — what this means for the reader's situation
5.Close — a direct statement, not a summary
Do not write a balanced "here are both sides" piece. Thought leadership takes a position.
Content Type
Educational / How-To
Organised by what the reader needs to understand in what order. Each section solves one part of the problem.
1.Opening — name the problem the reader is experiencing
2.Context — why this problem exists or why it matters
3.Core steps or concepts — in the order the reader needs them
4.Common mistakes or exceptions — what trips people up
5.Close — what to do first when the reader finishes
Do not add sections because the topic has more to say. Add sections because the reader needs them to solve the problem.
Content Type
Listicle — Middle Funnel
Each item in the list must carry real evaluation weight. A list of ten things with one sentence each is not a listicle — it is a padded outline.
1.Opening — the specific decision or problem the list addresses
2.Evaluation criteria — how you're assessing each item (stated upfront)
3.The list — each item with name, brief description, and specific evidence
4.Close — how to choose between them, or what to do next
Do not pad the list to hit a number. If seven items are honest, write seven. If three need more depth, write three with depth.
→
What your outline must contain for each section: The section heading, a one or two sentence description of what that section covers and argues, the key point or evidence it will use, and — for thought leadership — the logical connection to the next section. Headings alone are not an outline.
05 Fifth

Get the Outline Approved

Content head reviews before you write a word of the draft

Send the outline to the content head before drafting. Do not begin writing until you have sign-off. A rejected outline after a full draft has been written is a full draft's worth of wasted work.

What the content head is checking
QA & Factual Correctness
What they review
▸Whether the angle is factually accurate and not based on a misunderstanding of the topic
▸Whether the claims in the outline can be substantiated
▸Whether the content type and structure fit the author profile and publication
▸Whether the section logic holds — does the outline build toward something, or does it just list?
What to include when you submit
▸The target publication and URL
▸The assigned author
▸The keyword and your defined angle (one sentence)
▸The full outline with section descriptions — not just headings
▸Your planned sources for any factual claims
⚑
If the outline is sent back with feedback, resolve the feedback fully before resubmitting. Do not resubmit with partial changes and assume the rest is fine.
06 Sixth

Write the Draft

From approved outline to publish-ready content

Write against the approved outline. Every section should deliver what the outline promised. Do not add new sections mid-draft because you thought of something — if the section wasn't in the approved outline, it wasn't approved. If something genuinely important emerges, flag it before adding it.

Rule 1
Write in the author's voice throughout — not yours
You are a ghostwriter. The reader will believe this is the author's own thinking and experience. Every sentence should pass this test: would this author say this, in this way? If not, rewrite it until it does.
Rule 2
Every factual claim needs a source you have verified
Do not write "studies show" without a specific study. Do not cite a statistic you found on a secondary site — find the original source. If you can't verify a claim, cut it or rewrite it as the author's observation rather than a cited fact.
Rule 3
Follow the publication's structural conventions exactly
If the publication uses short paragraphs, use short paragraphs. If it doesn't use bullet points, don't use bullet points. You documented these in Step 1. Apply them in the draft.
Rule 4
The draft is publish-ready when you submit it
Your draft should need no copyediting for language. Proofread it before you send. Check: no typos, no broken sentence structures, no placeholder text, no unverified claims, no structural deviations from the outline.
Rule 5
ProofHub mentions, where included, must be natural
If ProofHub is referenced, it must fit the context and not read as a forced insertion. A mention that feels promotional in an educational article undermines the piece and the author's credibility on that publication.
Rule 6
Stay within the publication's word count range
You established this in Step 1. Do not submit a 2,000-word article to a publication that publishes 800-word pieces. Length is a structural convention, not a preference.
07 Reference

Platform Reference

Format and convention facts for LinkedIn, Medium, and guest posts

Keep this section open while drafting to check platform-specific requirements. These are the constraints that don't change regardless of the publication or content type.

LinkedIn
Social Long-Form
Length
Posts: 150–300 words. Articles: 800–1,500 words. Long posts get cut off at ~210 characters — the first two lines must hook.
Structure
Short paragraphs — one to two sentences each. Single line breaks between paragraphs. No horizontal rules. Bullet points are acceptable but use sparingly.
Opening
The first sentence must stand alone as a reason to keep reading. It will appear in the preview. Questions, bold claims, or unexpected statements perform best.
Tone
First-person, direct, conversational but professional. LinkedIn rewards personal framing — "I learned," "we decided," "here's what changed."
Closing
End with a direct statement or a single open question to encourage comments. Do not end with a list of hashtags in the body text.
Links
LinkedIn suppresses reach on posts with external links. Place any link in the first comment, not in the post itself.
Medium
Long-Form Article
Length
800–2,000 words for most articles. Aim for a 5–7 minute read. Medium shows estimated read time — use it as your calibration.
Structure
Subheadings (H2) every 300–400 words. Short paragraphs. Medium renders markdown — use it. Pull quotes work well for key statements.
Opening
No subheading in the first section — begin with a paragraph directly. Medium readers decide in the first two paragraphs. Get to the point.
Tone
Conversational but substantive. Medium readers are educated and will notice vague generalisations. Back claims with specifics.
Images
One cover image is expected. In-body images are optional. Caption all images. Do not use stock photos without checking licensing.
Links
Link naturally within the text where relevant. Medium does not penalise external links. Avoid linking to low-authority or promotional-only pages.
Guest Posts
External Publication
Length
Determined by the publication — check their guidelines page first. If no guidelines exist, match the length of their existing articles (Step 1).
Structure
Follow the publication's conventions exactly. Do not impose your own structure on a publication with an established editorial style.
Author bio
Most publications require a short author bio (50–100 words) on submission. Use the relevant author profile from Step 3 — condense it to fit the publication's limit.
Links
Most publications allow one to two links back to ProofHub. Place them where they add genuine context — not as sign-off links or forced insertions. Check the publication's link policy before submitting.
Submission
Follow the publication's submission format exactly — Google Doc, Word, or their own CMS. Formatting errors in submission create a poor first impression.
Originality
Guest posts must be original and unpublished. Do not repurpose or substantially reuse content from ProofHub's own blog or another submission.
